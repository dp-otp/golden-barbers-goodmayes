Always bring a reference photo — verbal descriptions lose information, a photo is exact. Know what you're asking for.

A French crop: short textured top, fringe forward, fade on sides. A comb over: length swept to one side with a hard part, skin fade under. A quiff: volume pushed up and forward, needs pomade or clay. The Art
of Fades

A fade is a seamless gradient — longer on top, shorter at the sides, disappearing into skin. The starting height defines the character. The blend zone — multiple overlapping passes with guards 0.5 through 2 — is where skill shows. A visible step means a rushed barber.

How to tell a good fade: Run your eyes down the side of your head. You should see a completely smooth gradient with no lines or steps. If your fade looks grown out after one week, the blend was started too high. Ask for it to start lower next time — you can always go higher, but you can’t undo a choppy blend.

Chapter 4

Beard
Grooming

A beard has three defining lines. Lose any one and the whole thing looks unkempt regardless of length. The neckline, the cheek line, and the moustache edge are what turn a beard into a groomed beard.

Neckline: Two fingers above the Adam's apple — that's where it sits. Lower than that and it blurs into the chest. Your barber razor-cleans this every visit.

Beard oil: 2–4 drops to damp skin after washing. Argan or jojoba oil softens coarse hair and moisturises the skin underneath, stopping beardruff and itch. Follow with a boar bristle brush to distribute and train the hair flat.

For longer beards, a beard balm provides light hold. Apply after the oil, comb through, and shape.

The most common beard mistake: letting the neckline grow too low. A neckline that sits at or below the Adam’s apple blurs into the chest and kills the shape entirely. Two fingers above the Adam’s apple is the correct position — ask your barber to clean this every single visit, even if you’re growing length.

Chapter 5

Ear
Flaming

Ear flaming — also known as ear singeing — is a traditional Turkish barbershop technique. A lit cotton wick soaked in a spirit solution is passed rapidly across the outer ear and canal entrance to burn away unwanted hair. The flame is extinguished in under a second and never contacts the skin directly.

The brief heat does two things: it burns the visible hair cleanly, and it cauterises the follicle opening, which significantly slows regrowth compared to waxing or tweezing. Most clients need it done every 6–8 weeks rather than the 2–3 week cycle of waxing. There is no skin irritation because no chemicals or adhesives are involved.

The sensation is a mild warmth and a faint crackling sound. The procedure takes under 5 minutes and is added to the end of any haircut or beard trim with no additional preparation needed.

Why it’s worth it: It targets the fine vellus hairs on the outer ear that clippers physically cannot reach. If you’ve never had it done, it takes under 5 minutes and costs very little as an add-on. Just ask for it at the end of your next haircut — most clients who try it once book it every time.

Chapter 6

Hot Towel
Shave

A hot towel shave is a six-step ritual. Each step serves a specific function — skip any one and the result degrades.

1. Hot towel (2–3 min): Opens pores, softens beard hair, and relaxes the facial muscles. This is what makes the razor glide rather than drag. 2. Pre-shave oil: Creates a protective slip layer between the blade and skin barrier — without it, even a sharp razor causes micro-abrasion. 3. Badger brush lather: Lifts every hair away from the skin so the blade cuts at the root rather than bending the hair flat. 4. Straight razor: First pass with the grain, second pass across the grain for a closer finish. A skilled barber reads your facial contours and adjusts the blade angle continuously. 5. Cold towel: Closes pores back down. Skipping this step leaves pores open and vulnerable to ingrown hairs. 6. Aftershave balm (not splash — alcohol strips moisture): seals and soothes. The full ritual takes 30–45 minutes.

What to use after: Skip the alcohol splash — it strips moisture from freshly shaved skin and causes the tightness and dryness many people associate with shaving. Use a balm instead. It seals without stripping. Also: the cold towel at the end is not optional. It closes the pores the hot towel opened. Miss that step and your skin is left exposed to bacteria and ingrown hairs for the next 24 hours.

Face
Treatments

Beyond the cut, Golden Barbers offer a range of face and skin treatments that leave you looking as sharp as your fade.

Wax treatments: Hot wax applied with a spatula removes unwanted facial hair — ears, nose, brows — cleanly and precisely. The wax adheres to the hair shaft, not the skin, making it far less irritating than threading or shaving those areas repeatedly.

Face masks: A charcoal or clay mask draws out impurities and excess sebum from pores. Applied after a hot towel open, left for 10–15 minutes, then removed — leaving skin noticeably cleaner and tighter. Ideal add-on after a shave when pores are already open.

Skin treatments at a barbershop are not a trend — they are maintenance. The same way a fade grows out, your skin reflects how well you look after it between visits.

Get more out of a face mask: Charcoal masks work best when your pores are already open. That’s why barbershops combine them with a hot towel treatment first. If you use one at home, hold a hot flannel against your face for 2–3 minutes before applying — you’ll get a noticeably deeper clean. Also, hard wax on the face sticks to the hair shaft, not the skin — far less irritation than strip wax. Ask specifically for it if you’re getting brows or nose done.

Colour &
Creative
Cuts

Achieving bold colour on dark hair is a multi-step process, not a single appointment. Dark or black hair must be pre-lightened with bleach first, then toned to neutralise brassiness, then the colour is deposited. Very dark hair may need 2–3 sessions to reach the target lightness without breaking the hair structure. Conditioning treatments between sessions are essential.

Carvings are shaved into the fade using a clipper at zero guard or a precision razor. Geometric shapes, lines, initials, even logos are achievable. They last 2–3 weeks before the growing hair blurs the edges. Maintenance visits keep them sharp. Always bring a reference image — the more specific, the more accurate the result.

What to know before booking colour: Bleach on dark or black hair doesn’t go blonde in one session — it goes orange first. That orange is called the undertone, and it needs a toner to neutralise it into platinum or ash. If any barber tells you they can do platinum in one session on natural black hair, either they’re rushing it or your hair will break. Two to three sessions is normal, and that’s a good sign, not a problem.

Chapter 10

Hair Health
& Hair Loss

Hair loss in men is almost always androgenetic alopecia (male pattern baldness) — a genetic condition where DHT (dihydrotestosterone) binds to follicles and progressively miniaturises them until they stop producing hair. It is not caused by hats, shampooing, or stress. It is genetic, and the earlier you act, the more you preserve.

The Norwood scale runs from Stage 1 (full coverage) to Stage 7 (horseshoe rim only). Recession at the temples is Stage 2–3 — still very treatable. Thinning at the crown is Stage 3 vertex. If you’re noticing either, start now.

Minoxidil (Regaine / Rogaine): applied directly to the scalp, it widens blood vessels and extends the anagen (growth) phase of the hair cycle, reactivating miniaturised follicles. Use the 5% concentration — the 2% version is significantly less effective for men. Foam formulation is preferred over liquid (less scalp irritation, easier to apply, dries faster). Apply once in the morning and once at night to a dry scalp — use the dropper to target the thinning zones, not the whole head. Results appear at 4–6 months. Stopping reverses gains within 3–6 months, so this is a long-term commitment. Available from Boots, Superdrug, or Amazon without a prescription.

Finasteride (Propecia): a prescription oral tablet that blocks DHT at the source. More effective than minoxidil alone. Often stacked with minoxidil for maximum retention. Requires a GP or online clinic prescription (Numan, Manual, HIMS). If treatment isn’t for you — a tight skin fade or buzz cut makes thinning look deliberate. Some of the sharpest cuts in this shop are on clients who embraced it.

Two things most people get wrong with Minoxidil: First — apply it to the thinning zones only, not the entire scalp. It works where you put it. Second — do not stop expecting to keep results. The hair it preserved will shed within 3–6 months of stopping, and you’ll return to where you would have been. It is a maintenance commitment, not a course of treatment.

Chapter 11

The
Line-Up

A line-up — also called an edge-up or shape-up — is the process of defining the hairline at the forehead, temples, and sideburns using a straight razor or T-blade trimmer. It transforms the natural, irregular hairline into a precise, geometric edge. It’s the detail that makes a grown-out cut still look fresh.

The three points that define a line-up: The forehead edge (straight or curved), the temple corners (squared or rounded), and the sideburn taper down to the ear. All three must be symmetrical — even 2mm off on one temple is visible. A barber uses a razor for the sharpest possible edge; a trimmer gives a slightly softer line.

Line-ups are maintenance cuts. They need touching up every 1–2 weeks even if you’re growing your hair. A sharp hairline on a longer style is always cleaner than an untouched one.

The smarter way to maintain your cut: Most people wait until they need a full haircut before getting a line-up. That’s backwards. A line-up every 10–14 days makes a 3-week-old cut look fresh for half the price of a full cut. Your hairline is the first thing people notice up close — keeping it defined is the highest return maintenance you can do.

Chapter 2

Haircuts & Styles

The most important thing you can do before sitting in the chair: bring a reference photo. Barbers work visually. A photo eliminates ambiguity instantly. “Short back and sides, not too much off the top” means something different to every person who has ever said it. A photo is precise — description is not.

The Core Styles

A French crop keeps the hair short and textured on top with the fringe pushed forward, nearly always paired with a fade on the sides. It’s one of the most popular styles in the shop because it works across almost every hair type and face shape. A comb over sweeps longer hair to one side, typically with a hard part — a razor-sharp line shaved into the scalp — and a high skin fade underneath. The contrast between the length on top and the bare sides is what gives it its structure. A quiff pushes volume up and forward from the forehead, held with pomade or clay. It requires a minimum of 2–3 inches on top and a mid or high fade to have the right visual weight. A buzz cut is uniform length all over using a clipper guard — guard 1 is 3mm, guard 2 is 6mm, up to guard 8 which is 25mm. There is no fade, no styling — just clean, consistent length and a defined neckline.

Face Shape and What It Changes

Round faces need height on top and tight sides — a high fade with a textured crop creates vertical length. Square faces suit mid fades with textured tops that soften the jawline. Oval faces suit almost everything. Diamond faces (wide cheekbones, narrow chin) benefit from volume on top with a low fade. Oblong faces should avoid extra height — textured or wavy hair with a low to mid fade creates balance. When in doubt, show a photo of what you want and your barber will tell you if it works for your face.

Waves and Locs

360 waves are built by brushing damp hair daily in circular patterns and compressing overnight with a durag. It takes weeks of consistent work. A barber who understands waves cuts with the grain and uses a low fade or taper that doesn’t disrupt the pattern. Locs require specialist knowledge — not every barber handles them. Maintenance involves retightening the roots and shaping the overall silhouette.

Chapter 3

The Art of Fades

A fade is a seamless gradient where hair transitions from longer on top to progressively shorter as it moves down the sides and back, ultimately disappearing into bare skin. It is the most technically demanding aspect of any haircut, and the element where a great barber most visibly separates themselves from an average one. The same clipper can produce a beautiful fade or a disaster depending on the hands using it.

Low Fade

Starts just above the ear and neckline. The most understated and versatile of the fade types. It doesn’t draw attention to the sides — it simply cleans them up. A low fade pairs with virtually every style on top and suits every face shape. It’s the safest option if you’re unsure, and the easiest to grow out without looking unkempt.

Mid Fade

Starts midway up the side of the head. Creates a clear visual contrast between the sides and top without being aggressive. A mid fade gives crops and quiffs their characteristic shape — the contrast between the shorter sides and longer top is what makes those styles work. One of the most requested fades in the shop.

High Fade & Skin Fade

Starts at or near the temples, often fading all the way down to bare skin. Maximum contrast, extremely bold. The skin fade in particular creates a striking, sculptural silhouette that frames the face precisely. It requires the most maintenance — the sides grow noticeably within 2 weeks, and most clients with a skin fade book every 2–3 weeks to keep it sharp.

Drop Fade

A variation of any of the above where the fade line curves downward behind the ear rather than following a straight horizontal line. This creates a rounded, more flowing silhouette at the neckline. It softens the overall shape compared to a straight-line fade, and is particularly popular on afros and waves where the round head shape benefits from a complementary curved fade line.

Burst Fade

A specialised fade where the gradient fans out in a semicircular arc around and behind the ear, radiating outward like a sunburst. The rest of the back and sides are left longer, creating a dramatic halo effect. A burst fade is most common on mohawks, afros, and mullet-style cuts where the contrast is intentional and theatrical. It requires precise freehand clipper technique and is one of the more advanced cuts to execute well.

Taper

Often confused with a fade, a taper is technically different. A taper shortens the hair gradually from the top toward the neckline but stops before reaching skin — it blends to a close, tight length rather than disappearing entirely. The result is cleaner and more defined than leaving the sides untouched, but more conservative and less dramatic than a full skin fade. Tapers work on professional or conservative dress codes where a skin fade would look too sharp or too high-maintenance.

Temple Fade (Brooklyn Fade)

A precise fade applied only at the temples and around the hairline, leaving more length on the sides and back of the head. The key is the clean definition of the hairline at the forehead corners, creating a very sharp, angular frame for the face. Common in barbershops with a strong West Indian or Black American influence, and often combined with a line-up to define the hairline all the way across the forehead.

The Blend Zone: What to Look For

The blend zone is the gradient between the longer hair on top and the faded section on the sides. A properly blended fade has no visible lines, steps, or uneven patches — the transition should be imperceptible when looking at the finished cut. Achieving this requires multiple overlapping passes with progressively smaller clipper guards (typically 0.5, 1, 1.5, and 2), combined with freehand scissor-over-comb work at the transition point. The best barbers also follow the natural contour of the head rather than cutting in mechanical straight lines — heads are round, and a rigid straight-line fade will always reveal itself at certain angles. Always inspect the cut in natural light before you leave the chair.

Chapter 4

Beard Grooming

A beard is only as good as its lines. Three separate boundaries define any beard shape — the neckline, the cheek line, and the moustache edge. Lose any one of them and the beard looks unfinished, regardless of how thick or long it is. These lines are what separate a grown beard from a maintained beard.

The Neckline: The Most Important Line

Place two fingers horizontally above your Adam’s apple. That exact point is where your neckline should sit. Not lower. A neckline that drops below this level blurs the boundary between beard and neck, creating an unkempt, undefined base. A neckline placed too high (shaved up into the jaw area) shortens the beard visually and can make the face look rounder. Your barber will razor-clean this line precisely on every visit. At home, use a detail trimmer or cartridge razor — never scissors — to maintain it between appointments.

The Cheek Line

Follow your natural growth line. The most common mistake is shaving the cheek line too low or too straight — both remove mass from the upper beard and make it look thinner. A barber only tidies stray hairs above the natural line, not the bulk beneath it. A higher natural cheek line is an asset — don’t touch it.

Beard Lengths

Stubble (1–3mm): Grade 1 for clean stubble, grade 2 for heavier. Lines are essential — stubble without a clean neckline is just an unshaved face. Short beard (5–10mm): A barber fade into the haircut at this length looks sharp. Needs a dedicated trimmer at home. Medium (10–25mm): Beard oil and a brush are non-optional. Full/long (25mm+): Regular trims maintain shape as the beard grows — regular cutting actually keeps it looking longer by removing split ends.

Daily Beard Care

Apply 2–4 drops of beard oil to damp skin after washing — damp skin absorbs it better. Massage from root to tip and into the skin underneath. This stops beardruff, itch, and coarseness. Look for argan, jojoba, or sweet almond oil as the base ingredient. Avoid silicones (dimethicone) — they coat the hair without nourishing it and build up over time. Use a boar bristle brush to distribute evenly and train flat. For longer beards, add a beard balm after the oil for light hold.

Chapter 5

Ear Flaming

Ear flaming — also known as ear singeing — is a traditional technique rooted in Turkish and Mediterranean barbershop culture, practised for generations as a standard part of the full grooming ritual. It targets the fine vellus and terminal hairs that grow on and around the outer ear and at the entrance to the ear canal — hairs that clippers cannot reach and tweezing or waxing can irritate.

The Technique

A thin cotton wick or twist of cotton wool is dipped in spirit (typically ethanol) and lit. The barber moves it in a rapid, controlled motion across the outer ear surface and around the ear canal entrance. The flame is present for a fraction of a second over any one area — it never rests against the skin. The burning hair is immediately wiped away with a damp cloth. The entire process takes under 5 minutes per ear.

Why It Works Better Than Alternatives

Tweezing pulls hair from the follicle and causes significant irritation in a sensitive area. Waxing around the ear applies adhesive directly to delicate skin and can cause redness, swelling, and ingrown hairs. Threading, while effective for eyebrows, is not practical for the curved surface of the ear. Ear flaming avoids all of these issues: no contact with the skin, no adhesives, no pulling. The brief heat cauterises the follicle opening which slows regrowth — most clients find the cycle extends to 6–8 weeks versus the 2–3 week cycle of waxing.

Safety

Performed correctly by an experienced barber, ear flaming is completely safe. The spirit used burns at a low temperature and the wick is moved too quickly to transfer meaningful heat to the skin. The sensation clients describe is a subtle warmth and the sound of faint crackling as the hair burns. There is no pain, no redness, and no recovery time. The key word is “experienced” — this is not a technique to attempt at home. At Golden Barbers, it is performed as a standard service by barbers trained in the procedure.

Chapter 6

Hot Towel Shave

A hot towel shave is a six-step ritual. Every step has a specific mechanical purpose. Skip any one of them and the result degrades. This is why a barber shave produces a fundamentally different finish to anything you can achieve at home — it’s not the razor, it’s the preparation and the sequence.

Step 1 — Hot towel (2–3 min): Moist heat opens pores, softens beard hair, and relaxes facial muscles. This is why a barber shave is closer than any home shave — soft hair cuts far more easily than dry. Duration matters — 30 seconds isn’t enough.

Step 2 — Pre-shave oil: Creates a slip layer between the blade and your skin. Without it, the razor drags and creates micro-abrasions with every stroke. Even a cartridge razor at home performs better with pre-shave oil applied first.

Step 3 — Badger brush lather: The brush lifts every hair away from the skin surface. A hair standing upright is cut cleanly; a hair pushed flat by the razor springs back below skin level and becomes an ingrown. The brush prevents this entirely.

Step 4 — Straight razor: First pass with the grain, second pass across the grain for closeness. A skilled barber re-angles the blade continuously along the jawline, upper lip, and adam’s apple. The result: no stubble for 2–3 days.

Step 5 — Cold towel: Closes the pores the hot towel opened. Skip it and your skin is left exposed to bacteria and ingrown hairs for 24 hours. Non-optional.

Step 6 — Aftershave balm (not splash): Alcohol splash strips moisture and causes rebound oiliness. A balm with aloe and glycerine seals the surface and reduces redness without stripping.

Chapter 7

Colour & Creative Cuts

Colour work and creative carvings are specialised services that go beyond a standard haircut. Both require additional skill, precision, and in the case of colour — multiple stages that can span more than one appointment.

Hair Colour: The Process

Colour on dark hair is not a single-step service. Dark brown or black hair cannot take vivid colour (platinum, teal, red, orange) in one sitting. The hair must first be pre-lightened using a bleach powder mixed with a developer (typically 20 or 30 vol). The bleach opens the hair cuticle and strips the existing pigment, lifting the hair to a pale yellow or white stage. The higher the developer volume, the faster and more aggressively it lifts — but also the more damaging. Very dark or coarse hair may require 2–3 bleaching sessions, spaced at least 2 weeks apart with deep conditioning treatments in between, to reach the target level without the hair snapping.

After bleaching, a toner is applied to neutralise the underlying warmth. Bleached dark hair typically pulls orange or brassy yellow — an ash or violet toner counteracts this and brings the hair to a clean, cool blonde or grey base. Only then can vivid colour be deposited with an accurate result. Coming in without this context and asking for “platinum blonde in one session” on black hair sets unrealistic expectations — a good barber will always explain the staged process before starting. Come in with clean, dry hair, no silicone serums or heavy conditioners applied (these prevent bleach from penetrating evenly), and a specific reference photo.

Aftercare for Colour

Use a purple shampoo once a week to counteract the brassy yellow that develops as toner fades. Use sulphate-free shampoo for all other washes — sulphates strip colour fast. Deep condition weekly. Bleached hair is structurally weaker — use heat protectant before hot tools and reduce heat frequency.

Hair Carvings & Designs

Carved into a skin fade using a zero-guard clipper or razor. Lines, shapes, initials, logos — all achievable. They last 2–3 weeks before regrowth blurs the edges. Always bring a reference image showing exact design, scale, and placement.

Chapter 8

Products & Daily Routine

Using the wrong product is one of the most common grooming mistakes men make. The right product for your hair type and desired finish makes the difference between a style that holds all day and one that collapses by lunch.

The Golden Rule

Apply all styling products to damp (not wet) hair. Too much water dilutes hold and causes product to run. Hair that’s slightly towel-dried absorbs product evenly. Work the product between your palms first to emulsify it, then rake through the hair from root to tip before styling with a comb or brush.

Hair Clay
High hold, matte finish. Best for textured crops, faux hawks and messy natural styles. Work a pea-sized amount between palms until it warms, then rake through damp hair. Builds texture and separation without shine. Brands: Uppercut Deluxe, Layrite Cement, Reuzel Fibre.
Matte • High Hold
Pomade
Medium hold, high shine. The classic barbershop product for comb overs, slick backs and quiffs. Water-based pomades (Suavecito, Layrite) wash out easily and are best for everyday use. Oil-based (Murray's, Dax) hold longer but need a clarifying shampoo to remove fully.
Shine • Medium Hold
Sea Salt Spray
No hold, natural texture. Spray onto damp hair and scrunch or diffuse for beachy waves, tousled texture, or enhanced natural curl definition. Perfect for wavy and curly hair. Leaves a matte, undone finish. Use as a base layer under light wax for texture with minimal hold.
Natural • Texture
Beard Oil
Apply 2–4 drops to damp skin after washing. Softens coarse beard hair and moisturises the skin beneath, preventing itchiness, flaking (beardruff) and redness. Look for argan oil, jojoba oil or sweet almond oil as the base. Apply morning and night for best results.
Daily • Skin + Hair
Derma Roller
A micro-needling roller used on the scalp to stimulate blood flow and collagen production at the follicle level. Used before applying Minoxidil, it significantly increases absorption — studies show up to 4× improvement in uptake. Use a 0.5mm roller once a week on a dry scalp, then apply Minoxidil 30–60 minutes after.
Hair Growth • Weekly Use
Shampoo & Conditioner
Wash 2–3 times per week maximum. Daily washing strips the scalp’s natural oils, causing dryness and overproduction of sebum. Use a sulphate-free shampoo and always follow with conditioner from mid-lengths to ends. Leave conditioner for 2–3 minutes before rinsing.

Chapter 9

Hair Health & Hair Loss

Hair loss in men is almost always androgenetic alopecia — genetic male pattern baldness caused by DHT miniaturising follicles over time. It is not caused by hats, shampooing, or stress. The only variable you control is how early you act.

The Norwood Scale

Stage 1–2: hairline intact or slightly receding at temples. Stage 3: M-shape recession forming, crown may thin. Stage 4–5: front and crown thinning merge. Stage 6–7: only a horseshoe rim remains. Treatment works best at Stage 2–3. By Stage 5–6, treatments maintain what remains but can’t regenerate lost follicles. If you’re noticing change, act now.

Minoxidil

Applied to the scalp twice daily, minoxidil prolongs the growth phase of the hair cycle and reactivates miniaturised follicles. Always use the 5% concentration — the 2% version is significantly less effective for men. Foam formulation is preferred over liquid (less scalp irritation, no residue). Apply to a dry scalp — morning and night — targeting thinning zones only. Results begin at 4–6 months. Stopping reverses gains within 3–6 months — this is indefinite maintenance, not a course. Available from Boots, Superdrug, or Amazon without a prescription.

Finasteride

An oral tablet that blocks DHT at the source. More effective than minoxidil alone, and often stacked with it. Stops hair loss in over 80% of men. Requires a prescription — available via Numan, Manual, or HIMS without a GP visit.

SMP & Embracing It

Scalp micropigmentation (SMP) deposits pigment to replicate the look of a shaved buzz cut at any hair density. Permanent and immediate. Alternatively — a skin fade or grade 1 buzz cut on thinning hair looks intentional and sharp. Trying to cover thinning with a longer style does the opposite of what you intend. Some of the best cuts in this shop are on clients who committed to a clean buzz and never looked back.

Chapter 10

The Line-Up

The line-up — also called an edge-up or shape-up — is the process of defining the natural hairline into a precise, geometric edge using a straight razor or T-blade trimmer. It is one of the most requested standalone services at the barbershop, and one of the most underappreciated details of any haircut. A sharp line-up takes a grown-out or in-between cut and makes it look intentional and fresh.

What It Does

The natural hairline is irregular. A line-up replaces it with a clean, defined edge across three zones: the forehead line, the temple corners (squared for a bold look, rounded for a softer result), and the sideburns (tapered or blended into the fade). Tell your barber which you prefer before they start — not after.

Squared vs. Rounded Corners

Squared corners are a 90-degree angle at each temple — bold, architectural, most popular with high skin fades. Rounded corners give a softer curve, better suited to a receded temple or a more understated look. Neither is wrong — it’s your preference.

How Often

Line-ups grow out visibly within 1–2 weeks. A standalone shape-up takes 10–15 minutes and costs a fraction of a full cut. If you have a sharp line-up with a high skin fade, plan for the shop every 2 weeks. A clean hairline is the first thing people notice up close — it’s always worth maintaining.
