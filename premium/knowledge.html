of Fades

A fade is a gradient blend — hair goes from longer on top to progressively shorter down the sides until it disappears into the skin. The starting point of the fade defines the whole look.

The blend zone is where a barber’s skill truly shows. It takes multiple clipper guards, freehand scissor work, and a trained eye to make the transition seamless. A patchy or visible “line” is the mark of a rushed job.

A beard needs three separate lines to look intentional: the neckline, the cheek line, and the mustache edge. Miss any one of them and the whole beard looks unkempt regardless of how long or full it is.

Neckline: Place two fingers above your Adam’s apple — that’s where it should sit. Any lower and the beard looks like it’s swallowing your neck. Your barber will razor-clean this line on every visit.

Ear flaming — also called ear singeing or ear candling — is a traditional grooming technique where a lit cotton wick is passed rapidly near the outer ear and ear canal entrance to burn away unwanted hair. The flame is extinguished almost immediately and never touches the skin.

Shave

A hot towel shave is the closest shave you will ever get. The process follows a precise sequence: a hot towel is applied to the face for 2–3 minutes to open pores and soften the beard. Then a pre-shave oil is massaged in to create slip and protect the skin.

Chapter 2

Haircuts & Styles

The single most useful thing you can do before your appointment is bring a reference photo. Barbers work visually — a photo communicates in seconds what a verbal description takes minutes to get right and often still doesn’t. “Short back and sides” means something different to everyone. A photo is precise.

Know the Names

A crop (or French crop) is short and textured on top with the fringe pushed forward, typically paired with a fade on the sides. A comb over sweeps longer hair to one side, usually with a hard part and high fade. A quiff pushes volume forward and upward from the forehead. A buzz cut is uniform length all over with a clipper guard — the number you choose determines the length (0 is skin, 1 is 3mm, up to 8 is 25mm).

Face Shape Matters

Round faces benefit from height on top and tight sides — it lengthens the face. Square faces suit textured crops and mid fades that soften the jawline. Oval faces are the most versatile — almost every style works. Diamond faces suit styles with width at the temples. If you’re not sure, ask your barber — it’s literally their job to know.

Specialist Styles

Locs (dreadlocks) and waves are their own discipline requiring specialist experience, the right products, and consistent technique. Not every barbershop can do them well. Golden Barbers has specialist experience in both.

Chapter 3

The Art of Fades

A fade is a gradient blend — hair transitions from longer on top to progressively shorter as it moves down the sides and back, eventually disappearing into bare skin. It’s the most technically demanding aspect of a haircut and where a great barber separates themselves from an average one.

The Four Fade Types

A low fade starts just above the ear and neckline. It’s the most understated version — clean, classic, and suits every face shape. A mid fade starts midway up the side of the head, creating stronger contrast between top and sides. It works brilliantly with crops and quiffs. A high fade starts at or near the temples and often goes all the way to skin — maximum contrast, very bold, requires upkeep every 2–3 weeks. A drop fade is a variation where the fade line curves downward behind the ear, creating a sleeker and more stylised silhouette — popular with waves and afros.

What Makes a Good Fade

The blend zone is where skill truly shows. It requires multiple clipper guards (typically 0.5, 1, 1.5, and 2) used in overlapping passes, often combined with freehand scissor-over-comb work to create a seamless transition. A patchy or visible “step” line in the blend zone is the mark of a rushed or unskilled cut.

The best barbers also follow the head shape rather than cutting in straight lines — everyone’s head is different, and a fade that looks perfect on one person can look wrong on another if the technique is applied rigidly.

Chapter 4

Beard Grooming

A beard needs three separate lines to look intentional: the neckline, the cheek line, and the mustache edge. Miss any one of them and the whole beard looks unkempt regardless of how long or full it is. Getting these lines right is what separates a professionally maintained beard from one you’ve tried to manage yourself at home.

The Neckline

Place two fingers above your Adam’s apple and draw an imaginary line from ear to ear — that’s where your neckline should sit. Any lower and the beard starts to blur into your chest hair, losing all definition. Your barber will razor-clean this line on every visit, giving it the crisp edge that makes the whole beard look intentional.

The Cheek Line

Follow your natural hair growth pattern. The mistake most men make is shaving the cheek line too low or too straight — this removes bulk from the upper beard, narrowing and shortening the face. A barber will tidy stray individual hairs above the natural line without compromising the shape and fullness below.

Daily Beard Care

Apply 2–4 drops of beard oil to damp skin (not dripping wet) after washing. The oil works on two levels: it softens coarse beard hair which reduces scratching for the person on the receiving end, and it moisturises the skin underneath which prevents the dreaded beard itch and flaking (beardruff). Argan oil and jojoba oil are the two best base ingredients to look for on the label. Massage in thoroughly then use a boar bristle brush to distribute evenly and train the hair to lie flat.

A beard balm serves a similar purpose but also provides light hold for shaping stray hairs on longer beards. Apply as the last step, work through the beard with a comb, and shape to preference.

Chapter 5

Ear Flaming

Ear flaming — also known as ear singeing or ear candling — is a traditional grooming technique that has been practised in Turkish barbershops for generations. A lit cotton wick soaked in a spirit solution is passed rapidly near the outer ear and ear canal entrance to burn away unwanted hair. The flame is extinguished almost immediately and never touches the skin directly.

How It Works

The brief heat of the flame does two things. First, it burns the visible hair cleanly without irritating the skin the way waxing or tweezing can. Second, the heat cauterises the hair follicle, which significantly slows regrowth compared to mechanical hair removal. Most clients find they need it done every 6–8 weeks rather than the 2–3 week cycle of waxing.

What to Expect

The process is completely painless. Clients typically describe feeling a gentle warmth near the ear and hearing a faint crackling sound as the hair ignites briefly. The entire procedure takes under 5 minutes. No chemicals are used, there is zero skin irritation, and there is no downtime whatsoever. It can be combined with any haircut or beard trim as an add-on service.

It’s one of the most requested services at Golden Barbers and a perfect example of the kind of specialist technique that separates a proper barbershop from a walk-in chain.

Chapter 6

Hot Towel Shave

A hot towel shave is the definitive barbershop ritual — a full sensory experience that leaves your skin feeling immaculate. Every step in the sequence serves a specific function, and skipping any one of them degrades the result.

The Process, Step by Step

Step 1 — Hot Towel: A towel soaked in hot water is wrapped around the face for 2–3 minutes. The sustained heat opens the pores, softens the beard hair (making it easier to cut), and relaxes the facial muscles. This is what makes a barber shave so much closer than a home shave — the prep.

Step 2 — Pre-Shave Oil: A light oil is massaged into the skin to create slip between the blade and the face, and to provide a protective layer over the skin barrier. Without this, even the sharpest razor causes micro-abrasions.

Step 3 — Lather: A rich shaving cream or soap is worked into the beard using a badger hair brush. The brush lifts every hair away from the skin so the blade can cut it cleanly at the base rather than bending it flat.

Step 4 — The Straight Razor: The blade is drawn across the skin in short, controlled strokes. First pass goes with the grain, second pass goes across the grain for a closer finish. A skilled barber reads your facial contours and adapts the blade angle continuously. The result is a shave so close you can’t feel any stubble for 2–3 days.

Step 5 — Cold Towel: A cold towel is applied to close the pores back down and reduce any surface redness. This step is critical — skipping it leaves pores open and vulnerable to ingrown hairs and irritation.

Step 6 — Aftershave Balm: Not an alcohol-based splash (which strips moisture and burns) but a moisturising balm that soothes the skin and seals the finish. The entire ritual takes 30–45 minutes and is deeply relaxing.

Chapter 7

Colour & Creative Cuts

Colour and creative carvings are a form of self-expression that Golden Barbers take seriously. They require specialist skill, the right products, and crucially — a good reference image from the client.

Hair Colour

Achieving bold colour like platinum blonde, teal or vivid red on dark hair is a multi-step process. The hair must be pre-lightened (bleached) first using a developer and bleach powder, then toned to neutralise unwanted yellow or orange undertones, then the colour is applied. Very dark or black hair may require 2–3 separate sessions to reach the lightness needed without damaging the hair beyond repair. Conditioning treatments between sessions are essential. Always come with clean, dry hair and no silicone-based products applied beforehand.

Hair Carvings & Designs

Carvings are shaved into the fade using a precision razor or clipper blade at a zero guard. Lines, geometric shapes, initials and logos are all achievable. They typically last 2–3 weeks before the growing hair blurs the edges. Maintenance visits keep them sharp. Always bring a reference — the more detailed your image, the closer the result.

Chapter 8

Products & Daily Routine

Using the wrong product is one of the most common grooming mistakes men make. The right product for your hair type and desired finish makes the difference between a style that holds all day and one that collapses by lunch.

The Golden Rule

Apply all styling products to damp (not wet) hair. Too much water dilutes hold and causes product to run. Hair that’s slightly towel-dried absorbs product evenly. Work the product between your palms first to emulsify it, then rake through the hair from root to tip before styling with a comb or brush.

Hair Clay
High hold, matte finish. Best for textured crops, faux hawks and messy natural styles. Work a pea-sized amount between palms until it warms, then rake through damp hair. Builds texture and separation without shine. Brands: Uppercut Deluxe, Layrite Cement, Reuzel Fibre.
Matte • High Hold
Pomade
Medium hold, high shine. The classic barbershop product for comb overs, slick backs and quiffs. Water-based pomades (Suavecito, Layrite) wash out easily and are best for everyday use. Oil-based (Murray's, Dax) hold longer but need a clarifying shampoo to remove fully.
Shine • Medium Hold
Sea Salt Spray
No hold, natural texture. Spray onto damp hair and scrunch or diffuse for beachy waves, tousled texture, or enhanced natural curl definition. Perfect for wavy and curly hair. Leaves a matte, undone finish. Use as a base layer under light wax for texture with minimal hold.
Natural • Texture
Beard Oil
Apply 2–4 drops to damp skin after washing. Softens coarse beard hair and moisturises the skin beneath, preventing itchiness, flaking (beardruff) and redness. Look for argan oil, jojoba oil or sweet almond oil as the base. Apply morning and night for best results.

Chapter 9

Hair Health & Hair Loss

Hair loss affects 85% of men by age 50, and the vast majority of it is caused by androgenetic alopecia — male pattern baldness. It’s genetic, triggered by the hormone DHT (dihydrotestosterone) binding to follicles in the scalp and gradually miniaturising them until they stop producing hair entirely. It is not caused by wearing hats, shampooing too much, or anything else you might have heard.

Recognising It Early

The Norwood scale classifies hair loss from Stage 1 (full head of hair) to Stage 7 (only a horseshoe rim of hair remains at the sides). Most men first notice recession at the temples or thinning at the crown — that’s Stage 2 to 3, still very treatable. The earlier you act, the more follicles you can preserve.

Treatments That Actually Work

Minoxidil (Regaine) is the most widely used and clinically proven OTC treatment. Applied topically to the scalp twice daily, it works by widening blood vessels and increasing nutrient flow to hair follicles, re-activating miniaturised follicles and extending the growth phase of the hair cycle. Results take 4–6 months of consistent use to become visible. Stopping treatment reverses the gains within 3–6 months, so it requires long-term commitment.

Finasteride is a prescription oral medication that blocks the conversion of testosterone to DHT. It’s more effective than minoxidil but requires a doctor’s prescription and carries potential side effects. Often used in combination with minoxidil for maximum effect.

Scalp Micropigmentation (SMP) is a cosmetic tattooing technique that deposits pigment into the scalp to mimic the look of a buzz cut on a shaved head. It provides instant visual results and is permanent. Not a hair loss treatment but a highly effective cosmetic solution.

Embrace It

If you’d rather work with it than against it: a skin fade or buzz cut makes thinning look deliberate and sharp. Some of the best-looking haircuts in the shop are on clients with thinning hair who have committed to owning it.
